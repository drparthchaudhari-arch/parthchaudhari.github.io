Case Study 1

Canine Congestive Heart Failure

URL: https://parthchaudhari.com/bridge/case-studies/canine-chf.html

← Back to Study Games

Signalment

  • Species: Canine (Golden Retriever)
  • Age: 8 years
  • Sex: Neutered Male
  • Presenting Complaint: Chronic cough and exercise intolerance

History

The owner reports a dry, non-productive cough that has gradually worsened over six weeks and is most noticeable at night or after mild activity. The dog previously completed 30-minute walks comfortably but now stops after 10 minutes and shows increased respiratory effort. Appetite is slightly reduced, and the owner notes occasional restlessness during sleep.

No recent travel or boarding history was noted, and vaccinations are current. The patient was diagnosed with a grade II left apical systolic murmur one year ago but was not started on cardiac medication at that time due to minimal clinical signs. The owner now seeks re-evaluation because coughing episodes are increasing in frequency.

Physical Exam Findings

  • Heart rate 148 bpm with occasional irregular beats
  • Grade IV/VI left apical systolic murmur
  • Respiratory rate 44 breaths/min at rest with mild increased effort
  • Fine crackles auscultated over caudodorsal lung fields
  • Mucous membranes pink, CRT under 2 seconds, body condition score 5/9

Diagnosis and Treatment

Thoracic radiographs showed left atrial enlargement with perihilar to caudodorsal interstitial-to-alveolar pulmonary infiltrates, supporting left-sided congestive heart failure. Echocardiography findings were consistent with myxomatous mitral valve disease and volume overload of the left atrium and ventricle.

Initial treatment included furosemide for pulmonary edema control, pimobendan for inotropic and vasodilatory support, and an ACE inhibitor as part of chronic management. Home monitoring instructions included resting respiratory rate tracking, exercise moderation, and early reassessment if cough or dyspnea worsened.

Key Learning Points

  • Night cough plus exercise intolerance and crackles strongly suggest left-sided CHF in dogs with known murmurs.
  • Thoracic radiographs remain essential to distinguish cardiogenic pulmonary edema from primary airway disease.
  • Pimobendan and diuretics are cornerstones of symptomatic CHF stabilization in degenerative mitral valve disease.
  • Owner education on resting respiratory rate provides practical relapse detection between visits.
