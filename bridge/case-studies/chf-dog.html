Canine Congestive Heart Failure (CHF)

Signalment and Presenting Complaint

  • BreedGolden Retriever
  • Age8 years
  • SexNeutered Male
  • Weight33 kg
  • Presenting ComplaintChronic cough and exercise intolerance

History

The owner reports a 6-week progression of dry nighttime coughing and reduced willingness to exercise. The patient previously completed regular long walks, but now stops early and pants more heavily after mild activity. Appetite is mildly reduced and sleep appears fragmented by episodes of restlessness and intermittent coughing.

A left apical systolic murmur had been identified on a prior annual exam, but no medication was initiated because the dog had no overt clinical signs at that time. Vaccination and preventive care are current, and there is no recent travel history or known infectious respiratory exposure.

Physical Exam

  • Temperature: 38.7 C
  • Pulse: 148 bpm, occasional irregular beats
  • Respiration: 44 breaths/min, mild tachypnea at rest
  • Grade IV/VI left apical systolic murmur
  • Fine caudodorsal lung crackles on auscultation
  • Mucous membranes pink, CRT under 2 seconds

Problem List

  1. Progressive cough with exercise intolerance
  2. Left apical systolic murmur with tachycardia
  3. Tachypnea and pulmonary crackles
  4. High suspicion for cardiogenic pulmonary involvement

Differentials (Ranked)

  1. Left-sided congestive heart failure secondary to degenerative valvular disease
  2. Primary chronic airway disease (e.g., chronic bronchitis)
  3. Pulmonary neoplasia or other lower respiratory pathology

Treatment Overview

Initial management focuses on reducing pulmonary congestion, stabilizing ventilation, and improving cardiac forward flow with evidence-based heart failure treatment classes. Ongoing outpatient management includes activity adjustment, respiratory-rate monitoring at home, and serial reassessment of cardiopulmonary status.

Key Learning Points

  • Signalment plus auscultation findings should drive rapid cardiopulmonary triage.
  • Thoracic imaging is central to confirming active congestive failure versus primary airway disease.
  • Clinical reasoning should connect murmur location, respiratory signs, and imaging findings.
  • Serial monitoring is essential because CHF control depends on dynamic clinical response.
