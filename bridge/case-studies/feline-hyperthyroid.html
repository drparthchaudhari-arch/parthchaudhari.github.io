Endocrine Case

Feline Hyperthyroidism

URL: https://parthchaudhari.com/bridge/case-studies/feline-hyperthyroid.html

← Back to Clinical Case Studies

Signalment and Presenting Complaint

  • SpeciesDomestic Shorthair Cat
  • Age12 years
  • SexMale Castrated
  • Weight4.2 kg
  • Presenting ComplaintWeight loss despite persistent appetite

History

Over three months, the owner has observed progressive weight loss despite a strong appetite. Activity and vocalization have increased, with intermittent restlessness at night.

Water intake is mildly increased and the patient appears less tolerant of handling than baseline. No recent dietary changes were reported, and vaccination/preventive care is current.

Physical Exam Findings

  • Thin body condition with reduced epaxial muscle mass
  • Tachycardia with resting heart rate around 220 bpm
  • Palpable thyroid slip in cervical region
  • Soft systolic murmur and elevated systolic blood pressure
  • Mild unkempt coat quality and anxious demeanor

Problem List

  1. Weight loss despite maintained appetite
  2. Tachycardia and cardiovascular strain
  3. Palpable thyroid enlargement
  4. Systemic hypertension risk

Differentials (Ranked)

  1. Feline hyperthyroidism
  2. Chronic kidney disease with secondary weight loss
  3. Diabetes mellitus

Diagnostics Plan

  • Confirm endocrine diagnosis with total T4 and repeat when suspicion remains high.
  • Check systolic blood pressure and retinal status for hypertension-related injury.
  • Use CBC/chemistry and urinalysis to assess renal and metabolic comorbidity.
  • Baseline cardiac assessment supports safe treatment planning in tachycardic patients.

Treatment Plan

Management options include antithyroid medication, dietary therapy, radioiodine, or surgery depending on case constraints. Blood pressure, renal parameters, and body weight should be monitored closely during stabilization and follow-up.

Outcome and Learning Points

Appetite and activity stabilized after treatment initiation, with improved weight trend on follow-up. The case emphasizes balancing endocrine control with renal and cardiovascular monitoring.

  • Senior cats with weight loss and tachycardia should be screened for thyroid disease early.
  • Total T4 is confirmatory in most typical presentations.
  • Hypertension and cardiac findings influence urgency and treatment planning.
  • Reassessment is essential because comorbidities may emerge during therapy.
