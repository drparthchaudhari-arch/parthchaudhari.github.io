Case Study 2

Feline Hyperthyroidism

URL: https://parthchaudhari.com/bridge/case-studies/feline-hyperthyroid.html

← Back to Study Games

Signalment

  • Species: Feline (Domestic shorthair)
  • Age: 12 years
  • Sex: Male Castrated
  • Presenting Complaint: Weight loss with polyuria and increased appetite

History

The owner reports a three-month history of gradual weight loss despite a strong appetite. Water intake and litter box urine volume have increased noticeably over the same period. The cat has become more vocal at night and occasionally vomits shortly after eating. No known toxin exposure or major diet changes were reported.

Indoor lifestyle and routine vaccinations are current. The cat has no previous diagnosis of diabetes or kidney disease. Activity level appears increased compared with prior visits, and the owner describes intermittent restlessness and reduced tolerance for handling around the neck region.

Physical Exam Findings

  • Body condition score 3/9 with notable muscle loss over the epaxial region
  • Heart rate 220 bpm with a grade II/VI systolic murmur
  • Palpable thyroid slip on the right cervical region
  • Systolic blood pressure 185 mmHg
  • Coat appears unkempt with mild dehydration

Diagnosis and Treatment

Laboratory work showed elevated total T4 with mild ALT increase and borderline azotemia. Combined with thyroid palpation, tachycardia, and weight loss despite polyphagia, the diagnosis was feline hyperthyroidism with systemic cardiovascular impact, including hypertension.

Initial management included oral methimazole with blood pressure control and close monitoring of renal parameters after thyroid normalization. Long-term options discussed with the owner included radioiodine therapy and nutritional management strategies, emphasizing that kidney function should be reassessed after achieving euthyroid status.

Key Learning Points

  • Weight loss with increased appetite in senior cats should always trigger thyroid screening.
  • Hypertension is a common and clinically important comorbidity in hyperthyroid cats.
  • Renal values can change after treatment, so follow-up chemistry monitoring is mandatory.
  • Definitive therapy decisions should balance owner goals, comorbidities, and long-term compliance.
