Feline Chemistry Normal Values: Context-First Study Reference

This feline chemistry reference is designed for rapid review during NAVLE prep and case-based learning sessions.

It focuses on values that commonly influence triage, renal assessment, endocrine reasoning, and fluid decisions.

Always interpret chemistry in combination with hydration status, urinalysis, and the patient timeline.

Last reviewed: February 13, 2026

Reference Table

Chemistry Parameter Typical Cat Range Clinical Use Note
Creatinine 0.8-2.4 mg/dL Trend with urine concentration and body condition for renal staging accuracy.
BUN 16-36 mg/dL Interpret with hydration and GI bleed context before assigning renal etiology.
ALT 20-100 U/L Persistent elevation requires hepatobiliary context and trend evaluation.
ALP 10-90 U/L Cats often have lower baseline ALP, so moderate rises can still be meaningful.
Total Bilirubin 0.0-0.4 mg/dL Correlate with hemolysis, cholestasis, and appetite history.
Glucose 70-150 mg/dL Stress hyperglycemia is common; confirm with context before diabetes labeling.
Sodium 147-156 mmol/L Use with osmolality and hydration markers in fluid planning.
Potassium 3.4-5.6 mmol/L Prioritize ECG correlation in marked potassium derangement.
Phosphorus 2.5-6.0 mg/dL Track with renal markers and stage-dependent diet management.
Total Calcium 8.2-10.8 mg/dL Confirm significance with albumin and ionized calcium when needed.

How to Use This Guide

Use this table as a starting point for pattern recognition, then verify each value against your laboratory reference report. Range overlap between healthy and sick cats can occur, so clinical framing is essential.

In dehydrated or hypotensive patients, chemistry abnormalities may partly reflect perfusion state. Repeat values after stabilization can change management decisions significantly.

When assessing renal disease risk, combine creatinine, BUN, phosphorus, urine concentration, and trend trajectory. This integrated approach is more reliable than relying on one chemistry value.

For endocrine differentials, avoid over-calling diabetes from a single high glucose in stressed cats without corroborating signs or repeat confirmation.

Variability and Caveats

  • Stress hyperglycemia may confound endocrine interpretation in feline patients.
  • Hydration shifts can exaggerate azotemia or mask severity during initial intake.
  • Albumin and protein status can influence interpretation of total calcium values.
  • Analyzer differences and sample handling delays can alter chemistry reliability.

When to Escalate Immediately

  • Severe hyperkalemia with weakness, bradycardia, or ECG instability.
  • Rapidly rising creatinine or phosphorus with worsening urine output.
  • Marked bilirubin increase with systemic decline or neurologic changes.
  • Persistent severe glucose instability despite initial correction attempts.

Worked Interpretation Example

  1. A senior cat presents for weight loss and lethargy. Chemistry shows creatinine 2.6 mg/dL, phosphorus high-normal, and glucose 188 mg/dL.
  2. Given stress at presentation, glucose is not immediately labeled diabetic. Renal risk is prioritized with urinalysis and blood pressure integration.
  3. Repeat chemistry after stabilization confirms persistent azotemia with stable glucose trend, supporting renal-focused follow-up and staging plan.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming all feline hyperglycemia indicates diabetes without stress-context review.
  • Using creatinine in isolation without muscle condition and urine data.
  • Ignoring recheck chemistry after fluid and perfusion correction.
  • Failing to integrate blood pressure and target-organ risk in renal cases.
